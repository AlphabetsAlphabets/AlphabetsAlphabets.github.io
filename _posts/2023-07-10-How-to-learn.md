To learn something you need to do encoding and retrieval. Encoding is the process of remembering that information and retrieval is pretty obvious. When you do encoding you must do proper encoding. It is important to understand that changing the way you learn is going to be difficult. However, the long term gains outweigh the short term negatives this is called desirable difficulty learning.

# Proper encoding
To get ideas in your head whatever practice you partake in must contain these five things[^1].
- Synthesis
- Reorganisation
- Comparison
- Application
- Contexts

In higher order learning[^2] you should learn something deeply in order to understand it better. To deeply understand something instead of looking at something in isolation, you should look at how it relates to other ideas.

Let's say you learned about X and you thought, "Hey, this might be related to Y." You do some searching and you found out that it isn't related however during your digging you find out it's actually related to Z instead.

As you keep doing this linking you'll start to see a pattern. You'll soon come to realise that X and Z can be grouped together as they're related. A simple analogy is the field of engineering, there are lots of different engineers. The specific type of engineer let's say flight engineer is the specific topic. If creating relations between ideas and grouping similar ideas are done then you've done both re-organisation, comparison and synthesis.

Analysing different contexts or testing out different scenarios is important as well. Let's say you learned how to implement a new device that can crack an egg. What happens if you crack an egg in a way that isn't intended? The new method is the new context. Doing all of this satisfies good encoding.

## Retrieval
After doing all that, you should remember everything right? No, not really. While putting in the work to form links between ideas does help with retrieval of past ideas it doesn't do much for the idea you just learned, so, what you should do is practice. It is the application of an idea that helps helps with retrieval as you'll need to recall how to actually use that idea. If you learned a math formula, you should learn what each variable means and what values to plug in.

You can use 
1. Spaced practice  You practice problem X but you space it out maybe in 15 minutes chunks.
2. interleave practice. You practice problems related to idea X, then Y then Z one after the other. The point is that the category of problems aren't the same. Maybe you're doing logarithm problems then after a while you swap to linear algebra.
